Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) is a technique that combines information retrieval with large language models (LLMs) to produce more accurate and grounded answers.

How RAG Works:
1. A knowledge base is first indexed — documents are split into chunks and stored as vector embeddings in a vector database.
2. When a user asks a question, the system retrieves the most relevant chunks using similarity search.
3. The retrieved chunks are then passed as context to the LLM, which generates a response grounded in that context.

Benefits of RAG:
- Reduces hallucinations since the model is constrained to available context.
- Enables use of up-to-date or private knowledge without fine-tuning.
- Improves transparency by making sources traceable.

Limitations of RAG:
- Quality of retrieval directly impacts answer quality.
- If the context is missing relevant info, the model may still hallucinate.
- Chunking strategy and embedding quality are critical design choices.

Hallucination in LLMs:
Hallucination refers to when an LLM generates plausible-sounding but factually incorrect or unsupported information. This is a major challenge in production AI systems.

Semantic Similarity:
Cosine similarity between embedding vectors is commonly used to measure how semantically related two pieces of text are. A score close to 1.0 means high similarity; close to 0.0 means unrelated.

FAISS (Facebook AI Similarity Search):
FAISS is an open-source library for efficient similarity search over dense vector collections. It is widely used in RAG pipelines due to its speed and scalability.

Gemini Models:
Google's Gemini family includes models for text generation (Gemini 2.5 Flash) and text embedding (gemini-embedding-exp-03-07). Gemini 2.5 Flash is optimised for speed and cost-efficiency while maintaining strong reasoning performance.
